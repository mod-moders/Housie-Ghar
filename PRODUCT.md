# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Two-sided platform.

**Staff** — Superadmins, Admins/CFOs, Operators, and Agents. They run paid Housie events: scheduling games, managing agent wallets, overseeing live draws, and collecting payment from players via WhatsApp/UPI. They work primarily on desktop/tablet admin panels.

**Players** — Community members joining a game from their phone. Their job is simple: browse games, pick tickets, enter a Housie name, pay via WhatsApp, and watch the live draw. They expect no friction and no account creation.

## Product Purpose

Housie Ghar digitizes the beloved Indian community game of Tambola/Housie — bringing a cryptographically fair draw, real-time ticket boards, and a structured agent-operator-admin hierarchy to what is currently a hand-managed, cash-based neighborhood game. Success means a game organizer can run a session end-to-end from their phone, and a player can book and watch from theirs, without paperwork or trust gaps.

## Brand Personality

Festive · Warm · Trustworthy

The public surface should feel like a well-run community event — not a casino, not a startup. The admin surface should feel calm and reliable under pressure: an operator running a 200-person live draw needs clarity, not excitement.

## Anti-references

- **Generic SaaS** (Notion, Linear, Vercel dashboard): cold minimalism, neutral grays, sidebar-heavy layouts. The product has warmth and community identity — it should not feel like a dev tool.
- **Fantasy sports apps** (Dream11, MPL): aggressive neon gradients, high-pressure CTAs, betting-hall energy. Housie Ghar is a social game, not gambling infrastructure.
- **Government lottery portals**: dated, bureaucratic, untrustworthy. Players need confidence in fairness, not form fields.
- **Western Bingo aesthetics**: pastel pinks, dabber imagery, retirement-home softness. The cultural reference is Indian Tambola, not British Bingo.

## Design Principles

1. **The game is the hero.** Every UI decision — color, motion, copy — should feel like it's in service of the draw event, not the software. When numbers fall, it should feel like a moment.
2. **Warm confidence, not decoration.** The forest/gold palette carries cultural weight. Use it intentionally; don't festoon every surface with gold gradients. Restraint in ornament, boldness in hierarchy.
3. **Staff see through the noise.** The admin surfaces should reduce cognitive load during live events. Clear status, clear actions, no visual noise that competes with the draw feed.
4. **Mobile is the primary channel for players.** The public lobby and game room are phone-first. Admin is desktop-tolerant. Design mobile before desktop for player flows.
5. **Trust is earned by transparency.** Fill bars, countdown timers, booking IDs, draw sequences — make the mechanics visible. Players and staff should never wonder what's happening.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

No current hard requirements. WCAG AA contrast targets where practical, especially on player-facing surfaces. Mobile-first rendering for player flows. Admin surfaces optimized for desktop.
